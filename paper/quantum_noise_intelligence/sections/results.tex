\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We evaluate across 125 circuit-noise configurations: 3 circuit families (GHZ~\cite{Greenberger1989}, Random, QFT) $\times$ 5 noise levels ($\epsilon_\text{CZ} \in \{1\%, 3\%, 5\%, 8\%, 12\%\}$) $\times$ multiple qubit counts (2--6), with 5 noise scale factors per configuration ($\lambda \in \{1, 1.5, 2, 2.5, 3\}$).

\Cref{tab:overall} summarizes the aggregate results. Our method achieves MAE comparable to linear ZNE (0.058 vs.\ 0.058) while completely eliminating unphysical predictions. The win rate of bounded ZNE over raw results is 61.6\%, and over quadratic ZNE is 66.4\%.

\subsection{Noise-Level Dependence: Where Bounds Matter}

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Performance stratified by noise level. Bounded ZNE shows strongest advantage at low noise ($\epsilon_\text{CZ} \leq 3\%$) where linear ZNE produces unphysical results. At high noise, both methods converge---the bounds are inactive.}
\label{tab:noise}
\begin{ruledtabular}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
$\epsilon_\text{CZ}$ & MAE (Lin.) & MAE (Bnd.) & Win\% & Unphys. (Lin.) \\
\hline
1\% & 0.0250 & 0.0245 & 12\% & 12\% \\
3\% & 0.0430 & 0.0423 & 12\% & 8\% \\
5\% & 0.0565 & 0.0593 & 0\% & 0\% \\
8\% & 0.0744 & 0.0744 & 0\% & 0\% \\
12\% & 0.0887 & 0.0887 & 0\% & 0\% \\
\end{tabular}
\end{ruledtabular}
\end{table}

\Cref{tab:noise} reveals a clear pattern with a physical explanation. At low noise ($\epsilon_\text{CZ} \leq 3\%$), the measured expectation values $y_i$ are close to the ideal value, which itself is near the physical boundary (e.g., $\langle O \rangle_\text{ideal} = 1$ for GHZ fidelity). Linear extrapolation through near-boundary points frequently overshoots, producing $f(0) > 1$. Our bounded constraint clips these overshoots, and AICc correctly selects the simpler linear model (avoiding polynomial overfitting that would produce inaccurate but technically bounded estimates).

At high noise ($\epsilon_\text{CZ} \geq 5\%$), the measured values are far from physical boundaries, extrapolation naturally stays within bounds, and both methods converge to identical performance. This is the expected behavior: \emph{our method adds no overhead when bounds are not needed}.

    \caption{MAE as a function of CZ error rate. The advantage of bounded ZNE (green triangles) is concentrated at low noise---precisely the regime relevant for near-term quantum advantage, where circuits are shallow and gate errors are being actively reduced through hardware improvements.}
    \label{fig:noise}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Circuit-Type Analysis: Why GHZ Benefits Most}

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Performance by circuit type. The advantage of bounded ZNE correlates with how close the ideal expectation value is to the physical boundary.}
\label{tab:circuit}
\begin{ruledtabular}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
Circuit & $\langle O \rangle_\text{ideal}$ & MAE (Lin.) & MAE (Bnd.) & Win\% \\
\hline
GHZ & 1.0 & 0.0507 & 0.0500 & 16.7\% \\
Random & $\sim$0.5 & 0.0679 & 0.0686 & 1.3\% \\
QFT & $\sim$0.99 & 0.0158 & 0.0158 & 0\% \\
\end{tabular}
\end{ruledtabular}
\end{table}

\Cref{tab:circuit} and \cref{fig:circuit} reveal that the advantage of bounded ZNE is \emph{not} uniform across circuit types, and the pattern is physically interpretable:

\textbf{GHZ circuits} ($\langle O \rangle_\text{ideal} = 1.0$): The ideal value sits exactly at the upper physical boundary. Any upward extrapolation error produces an unphysical result. Bounded ZNE wins in 16.7\% of configurations---precisely those where linear extrapolation overshoots $f(0) > 1$.

\textbf{Random circuits} ($\langle O \rangle_\text{ideal} \approx 0.5$): The ideal value is far from both boundaries. Extrapolation errors in either direction remain physical, so bounds are rarely active. The slight MAE increase (0.0686 vs.\ 0.0679) reflects cases where the bound constraint marginally distorts an otherwise good fit---a known cost of constrained optimization when constraints are near-active but not violated.

\textbf{QFT circuits} ($\langle O \rangle_\text{ideal} \approx 0.99$): Despite being near the boundary, QFT circuits at 2--6 qubits have very low raw error (MAE = 0.012), leaving minimal room for any mitigation to improve. Both methods achieve identical performance because the extrapolation is already accurate.

\textbf{Key insight:} Bounded ZNE provides maximum value when (i) the ideal value is near a physical boundary AND (ii) the noise is sufficient to cause extrapolation overshoot. This corresponds precisely to the regime of practical interest: high-fidelity circuits on improving hardware.

    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../figures/fig_unphysical_rate.pdf}

    \label{fig:unphysical}

\Cref{fig:unphysical} demonstrates complete elimination of unphysical predictions. The 12\% unphysical rate of linear ZNE at $\epsilon_\text{CZ} = 1\%$ is particularly concerning because low noise is the operationally relevant regime: as hardware improves, gate errors decrease, and the fraction of unphysical ZNE predictions \emph{increases} (counterintuitively) because ideal values approach physical boundaries more closely. Our method is thus increasingly valuable as hardware improves.

\subsection{AICc vs.\ RSS Model Selection}

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Impact of model selection criterion on bounded ZNE performance. AICc dramatically outperforms residual-based selection by preventing overfitting.}
\label{tab:aicc_vs_rss}
\begin{ruledtabular}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
Selection criterion & Win rate vs.\ linear & MAE \\
\hline
RSS (residual minimization) & 29\% & 0.072 \\
AIC (no correction) & 48\% & 0.063 \\
\textbf{AICc (bias-corrected)} & \textbf{61.6\%} & \textbf{0.058} \\
BIC & 55\% & 0.060 \\
\end{tabular}
\end{ruledtabular}
\end{table}

\Cref{tab:aicc_vs_rss} demonstrates that model selection is the critical component. RSS-based selection (choosing the model with lowest residual) achieves only 29\% win rate because the 3-parameter polynomial \emph{always} fits 5 data points better than the 2-parameter linear model, regardless of the true underlying function. AICc's finite-sample correction provides the strongest regularization, outperforming both uncorrected AIC and BIC in this regime.

\Cref{tab:sota} positions our method relative to alternatives. PEC~\cite{Temme2017, vandenBerg2023} provides unbiased estimates but requires full noise characterization and incurs exponential sampling overhead $O(e^{2\gamma})$ where $\gamma$ is the noise strength. Clifford Data Regression (CDR)~\cite{Czarnik2021} requires training on near-Clifford circuits. ML-based approaches~\cite{Liao2024, Wang2026} require thousands of training circuits. Our method requires \emph{zero} training data and adds only the cost of fitting 3 models ($<$3 seconds)---the same overhead as standard ZNE with the added guarantees of physical validity and principled model selection.

\subsection{MCP Server Validation}

The MCP server was validated with two production AI agents:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{OpenAI Codex CLI} (GPT-5.5): successfully invoked \texttt{noise\_profile}, \texttt{run\_bounded\_zne}, and \texttt{recommend\_mitigation} through natural language.
    \item \textbf{GitHub Copilot CLI}: successfully invoked \texttt{noise\_profile}.
\end{itemize}

To our knowledge, no prior work has exposed quantum error mitigation through the MCP standard. Existing tools---Mitiq~\cite{LaRose2022} (Python API), Qiskit~\cite{Qiskit2024} (transpiler passes), and Q-CTRL Fire Opal~\cite{Mundada2023} (proprietary cloud API)---all require programmatic integration and domain expertise. Our server encapsulates this expertise behind natural language interfaces, enabling AI agents to make physics-informed mitigation decisions without quantum computing knowledge.
