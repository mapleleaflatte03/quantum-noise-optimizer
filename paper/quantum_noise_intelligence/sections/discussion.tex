\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Regime of Applicability}

Bounded ZNE with AICc provides maximum value in a specific, physically motivated regime: observables whose ideal value lies near a spectral boundary, measured on hardware with low-to-moderate gate error. This is not a niche scenario---it describes the trajectory of NISQ development as hardware improves.

Two trends reinforce this. First, as gate errors decrease, measured values approach the ideal, which itself sits near a boundary for common observables (GHZ fidelity $\to 1$, ground-state energy $\to E_0$). Second, the extrapolation leverage $|f(0) - y_1|/|y_1|$ grows, increasing overshoot probability. Both effects make unphysical predictions \emph{more} likely on better hardware---a counterintuitive failure mode that our method specifically addresses.

For observables centered far from boundaries (e.g., $\langle Z_1 Z_2 \rangle \approx 0$ in random circuits), the bounds are rarely active and our method reduces to standard model-selected ZNE with no additional benefit or cost.

\subsection{Computational Cost}

Our method adds negligible classical overhead to standard ZNE:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Three L-BFGS-B fits: $<$50 iterations each, $<$1\,s total wall time.
    \item AICc evaluation: $O(1)$ per model (closed-form).
    \item No additional quantum circuit executions beyond standard ZNE.
\end{itemize}
The total classical cost ($<$3\,s) is negligible relative to quantum execution time on current hardware (minutes to hours per job). The method is thus ``free'' in the resource that matters---quantum circuit shots.

\subsection{Scalability}

The extrapolation step operates on scalar expectation values regardless of system size: fitting three models to 5 data points takes the same time whether the underlying circuit has 5 or 500 qubits. The scaling bottleneck is noise amplification (unitary folding), which is linear in circuit depth and already standard practice~\cite{GiurgicaTiron2020}.

For local observables, physical bounds $[-1, 1]$ apply at any scale. For global observables (e.g., state fidelity), bounds tighten exponentially with system size, potentially increasing the method's utility. The open question is whether our three candidate models adequately capture noise decay at scale, where spatially correlated errors~\cite{vandenBerg2023} may produce decay curves outside our model family.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{No hardware validation.} Simulator noise (depolarizing, Markovian) does not capture coherent errors, crosstalk, or drift present in real processors. Our 0\% unphysical rate may not hold under non-Markovian noise where the decay curve is non-monotonic.

    \item \textbf{Restricted model family.} Three candidate models cannot span all possible decay behaviors. Coherent error interference can produce oscillatory decay that defeats polynomial, exponential, and poly-exponential fits alike. Expanding the family risks overfitting unless AICc penalties scale appropriately.

    \item \textbf{Fixed scale factors.} Uniform $\lambda \in \{1, 1.5, 2, 2.5, 3\}$ may not optimally sample the decay curve. Adaptive designs~\cite{GiurgicaTiron2020} could reduce the number of required executions.

    \item \textbf{Bias when bounds are active.} When the unconstrained optimum violates a bound, clipping introduces bias toward the boundary. This bias is bounded by $|f_\text{unconstrained}(0) - l_b|$ (or $u_b$), but may systematically underestimate corrections for observables near boundaries.

    \item \textbf{No accuracy guarantee.} Theorem~1 guarantees physical validity but not accuracy. A bounded estimate can be arbitrarily inaccurate within $[l_b, u_b]$ if all candidate models are poor approximations to the true decay.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Comparison with State-of-the-Art}

\textbf{vs.\ Mitiq~\cite{LaRose2022}.} Mitiq delegates model selection to users via factory classes (\texttt{LinearFactory}, \texttt{ExpFactory}). Our AICc selector is a drop-in replacement: it accepts the same noise-amplified data and returns a zero-noise estimate, requiring $<$20 lines to integrate. The key difference is automation---our method eliminates a decision that currently requires expert judgment.

\textbf{vs.\ Q-CTRL Fire Opal~\cite{Mundada2023}.} Fire Opal combines pulse optimization, dynamical decoupling, and proprietary post-processing, reporting $>$1000$\times$ improvement on specific benchmarks. Direct comparison is methodologically inappropriate: their improvement reflects three stacked layers, while we address only the extrapolation step. More fundamentally, Fire Opal is closed-source and hardware-specific (IBM only), precluding independent verification or extension. Our method is complementary---one could apply Fire Opal's error suppression upstream and our bounded extrapolation downstream.

\textbf{vs.\ PEC~\cite{Temme2017, vandenBerg2023}.} PEC provides \emph{unbiased} estimates (stronger than our bounded guarantee) at exponential sampling cost $O(e^{2\gamma})$. For a 20-qubit circuit with $\gamma \approx 5$, this requires $\sim$$e^{10} \approx 22{,}000\times$ more shots than standard execution. Our method maintains polynomial overhead (same shots as ZNE) at the cost of potential bias when bounds are active. For most NISQ applications where shot budgets are limited, this tradeoff favors our approach.

\textbf{vs.\ CDR/vnCDR~\cite{Czarnik2021, Lowe2021}.} CDR achieves excellent accuracy under global depolarizing noise by learning a linear correction from near-Clifford training circuits. Its weakness is the $O(2^n)$ classical simulation cost per training circuit and degradation under correlated noise. Our method requires zero training data, making it applicable immediately on new hardware without a calibration phase.

\textbf{vs.\ ML-QEM~\cite{Liao2024, Wang2026}.} ML methods represent the accuracy frontier (demonstrated at 100 qubits~\cite{Liao2024}) but occupy a fundamentally different point in the cost-guarantee space: high accuracy, high training cost ($O(10^4)$ circuits), no physical guarantees, and retraining required when hardware drifts. Our method trades accuracy ceiling for zero-cost deployment and provable validity---appropriate for settings where reliability matters more than optimality.

\subsection{Risks of AI-Automated Mitigation}

Exposing quantum error mitigation to AI agents via MCP introduces specific risks that our design mitigates but does not eliminate:

\textbf{Over-reliance on mitigated results.} An AI agent may treat bounded ZNE output as ground truth without understanding residual error. Our server returns confidence metadata (AICc scores, model selected, residual magnitude) to enable agents to flag low-confidence results, but this requires the agent to interpret metadata correctly.

\textbf{Hallucinated parameters.} LLMs may invoke tools with fabricated inputs (e.g., requesting mitigation for a ``1000-qubit GHZ circuit'' that was never executed). Our server validates inputs against physical constraints (qubit count $\leq$ backend capacity, positive noise levels) but cannot verify that the claimed circuit was actually run.

\textbf{Strategy misapplication.} AutoMitigator's rule-based selection may recommend inappropriate strategies if the agent provides inaccurate circuit metadata. The rules are conservative (defaulting to bounded ZNE, which is always safe), but suboptimal recommendations waste computational resources.

These risks are inherent to any tool-using AI system and are not specific to quantum computing. Our mitigation strategy---embedding physical constraints in the tool rather than relying on the agent to enforce them---follows the principle of least privilege: the tool cannot produce unphysical results regardless of how it is invoked.

\subsection{Broader Context}

Our work sits at the intersection of three trends: (i) the maturation of quantum error mitigation from ad-hoc techniques to systematic frameworks~\cite{Cai2023, Endo2021}, (ii) the emergence of information-theoretic approaches to model selection in physics~\cite{Burnham2002}, and (iii) the rapid adoption of AI agents in scientific workflows~\cite{Anthropic2024}.

The combination is timely. As quantum hardware approaches the threshold where error-mitigated results become scientifically useful~\cite{Kim2023}, the demand for reliable, automated mitigation will grow. Methods that provide formal guarantees (physical validity) while requiring minimal expert intervention (automatic model selection) are better positioned for widespread adoption than those requiring per-circuit tuning or extensive training data.
